Problem Statement

The starting question was not "what should I build?" It was: "What tool exists that would actually match my study style — how I understand concepts?" The search for that tool came up empty.

Existing solutions — lecture slides, textbooks, YouTube, Google — deliver the same format to every learner and expect the same results. But understanding is not uniform. It depends on personality, emotional state, learning style, and the right analogy at the right moment.

"When I reached flow state in my studying, I realized how much I enjoy what I study. That became my motivation to keep going. I wanted to build something that creates students who actually understand — not just students who know."

Natasha Matare — Product Origin

A secondary observation reinforced the problem: a friend asked Natasha to input her notes into ChatGPT because she couldn't afford the Pro subscription. The barrier to AI-assisted learning wasn't awareness — it was access. Sensei was conceived as a solution that removes that barrier: accessible to any demographic, adaptable to any learner.

01

Technical Feasibility

PASS

Before committing to development, the technical requirements were inventoried against existing skills and identified gaps.

Capability
Frontend development, UI design, application architecture, hosting deployment
Risk Identified
AI integration via OpenAI API — unknown territory at project start
Resolution
Researched and implemented API integration mid-build; actively iterating

The frontend, design, and hosting requirements were within scope from day one. The critical unknown was AI integration — specifically, how to connect the OpenAI API to deliver personalised, context-aware study breakdowns. This was identified as the primary technical risk.

A secondary decision point emerged around the backend infrastructure: Firebase vs. Supabase. Both were evaluated against real-world evidence — a family member's existing Firebase implementation and peer adoption patterns pointing toward Supabase among developers of a similar profile. Firebase was selected as the more familiar and better-documented starting point, with the option to revisit as backend requirements become clearer in the development phase.

Verdict: All core capabilities confirmed. Primary risk (AI integration) identified early and being actively resolved through implementation and learning. Technical stack is viable.

02

Financial Feasibility

PASS

The financial assessment evaluated both the cost to build and the return on investment — not just in revenue terms, but in career and mission terms.

Cost to Build

  • Cursor Pro subscription (AI-assisted development)
  • ChatGPT Pro (prompt development and research)
  • OpenAI API usage costs
  • Backend hosting (Firebase — current free tier; paid tier planned)
  • Vercel deployment (free tier for MVP)

Return on Investment

  • Portfolio artifact demonstrating full SDLC execution
  • Internship readiness and competitive differentiation
  • Real product with active users — not a demo
  • Foundation for startup if capital raised post-internship
  • Mission return: accessible learning for all demographics

The ROI assessment concluded positively. Total build costs are manageable at the student budget level, and the returns — career positioning, a live product, and a scalable foundation — significantly outweigh the investment. The go-to-market strategy is staged deliberately: Phase 1 deploys via Vercel at no cost to users, building an initial user base and gathering feedback. Phase 2, contingent on securing employment and raising capital, transitions Sensei toward a commercially viable product.

Verdict: Financially viable at current stage. Costs are known and bounded. ROI is strong across career, product, and mission dimensions.

03

Operational Feasibility

PASS

Operational feasibility assessed whether real users would adopt and find value in Sensei as designed. This was validated through three channels.

Survey validation (51 respondents): Participants were presented with a description of Sensei's core functionality and asked whether they would use it. The overwhelming majority said yes. One respondent said no. Several said maybe — which itself is useful signal, pointing to areas where the value proposition needs sharpening.

Stakeholder interviews: Conversations with a medical student cousin and two brothers provided qualitative confirmation. The medical student's response was particularly instructive: "That's going to work for everybody — it's going to work for me personally." Medical students represent a high-demand, high-stakes learning context. If Sensei works there, it works across the demographic range.

Observed behaviour: A friend asking to have her notes manually entered into ChatGPT — because she couldn't afford the Pro subscription — validated both the demand and the access gap Sensei is designed to close.

"I believe when you understand something, you become the best version of yourself in that thing. We want to create students who actually understand what they are studying — not just students who just know."

Natasha Matare — Operational Vision

Verdict: Strong user demand confirmed across multiple validation channels. Accessibility is a core product principle — not a feature — which broadens the addressable user base to any learner regardless of economic background.

04

Schedule Feasibility

PASS

Schedule feasibility evaluated whether Sensei could be built and deployed within a meaningful timeframe alongside academic commitments and full-time employment.

The decision to use Cursor as an AI-assisted development environment significantly compressed the build timeline, allowing rapid prototyping and iteration without sacrificing code quality. This was a deliberate tool selection made during the planning phase — not a mid-build workaround.

The MVP scope was defined with schedule constraints in mind: core functionality first (file ingestion, AI breakdown, topic-by-topic questioning, progress gating), with advanced features (psychological comfort layer, scenario-based exams, full backend) staged into subsequent sprints. This phased approach — aligned with Agile methodology — ensures a working product can be deployed and iterated on without waiting for every feature to be complete.

Milestone plan: MVP functional prototype for portfolio and internship application season. Post-internship: capital raise, backend scaling, and public launch.

Verdict: Achievable within the defined timeline using a phased Agile approach. Scope is appropriately bounded for the current development phase.

05

Legal Feasibility

PASS

Legal feasibility confirmed that building and deploying an AI-integrated study application is permitted under applicable laws and platform terms of service.

Key areas reviewed: OpenAI API terms of service (commercial and personal use permitted within usage guidelines), data handling requirements for user-uploaded academic materials, and the absence of regulatory barriers to operating an EdTech product in the target markets.

Future legal considerations as the product scales: data privacy compliance (PIPEDA in Canada, GDPR if expanding to EU), terms of service for user-generated content, and age-appropriate data handling given the elementary-to-post-grad demographic range.

Verdict: No legal barriers to development or deployment at the current stage. Compliance requirements are well-understood and manageable as the product scales.

06

SDLC Methodology Selection

Following the feasibility assessment, the appropriate SDLC model was selected for development.

Agile — Selected

  • Iterative sprints with frequent feedback loops
  • Flexible scope — features can be added or cut based on user data
  • Matches the fast-paced, feedback-driven nature of the product
  • Allows MVP deployment before all features are complete
  • Aligns with real-world user research already underway

Waterfall — Rejected

  • Requires complete requirements upfront — not appropriate for a user-research-driven product
  • Sequential phases make it difficult to respond to user feedback mid-build
  • Higher risk of building the wrong thing before discovering it
  • Incompatible with solo development and academic constraints

The selection of Agile was not a default choice — it was a deliberate decision based on the nature of the product. Sensei's design is user-feedback-driven by definition: the survey data already reshaped the product direction once, and continued iteration based on real user behaviour is central to the product strategy. Waterfall's rigidity would have worked against that.

Overall Assessment

Sensei is feasible across all five dimensions. Proceed to system design.

Technical risks are identified and being actively resolved. Financial investment is bounded and ROI is strong. Operational demand is validated across multiple user segments. Schedule is achievable with a phased Agile approach. No legal barriers exist at the current development stage.

The deeper validation is not just that this can be built — it is that it should be built. The people who need it most are the people who currently can't afford the tools that would help them. Sensei is the answer to that gap.

VERDICT: FEASIBLE -- PROCEED TO SYSTEM DESIGN
